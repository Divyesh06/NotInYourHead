What happens inside the brain of a Depressed person?

For people who experience depression, the size of specific regions of the brain can decrease, the shrinkage may be reversible though. Following parts of the brain can be affected:

Hippocampus - It has a major role in learning and memory. Studies have shown that it also gets affected in a variety of neurological and psychiatric disorders.

Thalamus - It's involved in many high-level functions such as speech, thought, behavioural reactions, learning, and movement. Because it links sensory input to positive and negative feelings, depression can affect the thalamus, which can get smaller in those diagnosed with depression.

Amygdala - In depression, the amygdala is overactive and responds excessively to negative events. In turn, the amygdala connects to a set of brain regions that hone the physiological and behavioural response to emotional stimuli.

Prefrontal Cortices - They manage attention, impulse control, and emotional reactions. In brain-imaging studies using PET scans, depressed people display abnormally low activity in the prefrontal cortex.

What happens inside the brain of a person with Anxiety?


The brain is made up of two hemispheres, the right and the left. Both the sides are connected by a bundle of fibres. Communication between the right and left happens along these fibres, but during anxiety, the messages don't flow smoothly. The Amygdala and the Hippocampus play significant roles in most anxiety disorders

Amygdala - A structure deep in the brain that can alert the rest of the brain that a threat is present and trigger a fear or anxiety response.

Hippocampus - The part of the brain that encodes threatening events into memories.

Stress-Hormone Boost - Responding to the signals from the hypothalamus and the pituitary gland, the adrenal glands pump out high levels of the stress hormone cortisol.

In addition, certain parts of the brain become dominant and drive behaviour. This is evidence of a strong, healthy brain switching into survival mode, but during anxiety it happens too much and unnecessarily. It's not just the feeling of being restless rather some of the physical effects include a racy heart, sick stomach, clammy skin, vomiting, shaking of hands and legs.

All this is because of the surging of these neurochemicals, the natural end to the fight or flight response is intense physical activity. When there is no need to fight or flee, there is nothing to burn up the neurochemicals and they build up, causing the physical symptoms of anxiety. This is not under your control, and you should not be blamed for it, neither should you feel guilty for feeling the way you do. What should be normalized is asking for help, and then actually receiving it without experiencing all the stigma and discrimination, to not get shame for an actual illness that cannot be controlled. Mental Health Disorder is an actual concern around the globe.
